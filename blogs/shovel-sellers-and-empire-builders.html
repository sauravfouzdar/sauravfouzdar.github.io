Shovel Sellers and Empire Builders

On junk hardware, plywood servers, and the quiet pattern behind two tech booms, twenty-five years apart

By Saurav Fouzdar · June 26, 2026

A Chip, a Plywood Rack, and Twenty-Five Years

There's a small, strange detail buried in Sun Microsystems' history. In its prime, Sun's engineers reportedly looked at the cheap Intel chips running underneath Linux and called them "junk", fine for desktops, not for serious machines. Sun built its own processor, its own operating system, all fused together, all running the early internet's biggest names: eBay, Yahoo, Amazon. It was, by every visible measure, the better-engineered machine.

Around the same time, a few hundred miles away, two Stanford students were doing something almost embarrassing by comparison. Instead of buying serious servers, they were screwing bare motherboards onto plywood boards, the cheapest parts they could find, and writing software to babysit them. Their own internal description of the philosophy, years later, was almost funny in its plainness: "a reliable computing infrastructure from clusters of unreliable commodity PCs." They weren't trying to build a better machine than Sun's. They were trying to make the question of "better machine" stop mattering.

It's a curious thing to sit with, twenty-five years later: the company selling the obviously superior hardware lost almost everything, and the company gluing together junk on plywood became one of the most valuable on Earth.

The same quiet drama played out next door, in networking. Cisco built routers nobody else could match, and for a while that seemed like the whole game, until the same plywood-and-software company started building its own data-center networks out of cheap, interchangeable switches, arranged cleverly enough that the cheapness stopped being a weakness.

What's strange is watching the shape of that story show up again, almost exactly, in a completely different decade. There's a chipmaker today whose hardware nobody can quite replace, and a layer of software wrapped around it that customers have spent two decades learning to think in, a moat with real teeth, unlike Sun's. But underneath that confidence, the same instinct is stirring in the same kind of buyer: a few large companies quietly designing their own chips, the unmistakable shape of someone trying not to keep paying a premium they no longer think they need to pay.

And one floor up, where the AI labs live, there's a different kind of echo. A free, open alternative is creeping closer in quality to the expensive, closed one, not identical, but close enough that the question "why am I still paying for this?" is starting to get asked out loud, in roughly the same tone people once asked it about Solaris versus Linux.

None of this proves how the current story ends. The plywood-rack company didn't know it was right for years either, it just kept building, quietly, underneath everyone who was busy admiring the better-engineered machine. The pattern doesn't repeat on a schedule. It just seems to like rhyming.
